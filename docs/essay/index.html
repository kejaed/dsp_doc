Sonar

How a box on a boat knows there's a submarine four kilometres away, from first principles.

A long, slow read in twenty figures. Drag every slider.

Imagine a steel ball dropped into a still pond. A round ridge of water races outward from where it landed. The ridge carries no water with it — every drop in the pond stays roughly where it was — only the shape of the surface travels. When the ridge reaches the dock at the edge of the pond it slaps the wood, bounces off, and races back the way it came. If you'd been timing it, you could have measured the round trip and worked out how far the dock is.

That is, very nearly, sonar. There is no pond, no ball, and no wooden dock — but underwater there are pressure waves, and there is a transducer that fires them, and there are submarines and rocks and fish that bounce them back. Every step in what follows is a slightly more careful version of the pond.

Before any of that, though, we have to be precise about what is actually travelling between the transducer and the target. It is not water. It is a pressure wave: a moving pattern of squeezes and stretches in the medium. The figure below is a cartoon of one.

Two things are worth noticing here. First, although the pattern is moving steadily from left to right, no single dot ever drifts left or right — each one wobbles in place. Sound is a way for energy and information to cross a medium without carrying any of the medium with it. Second, the pressure profile is exactly the spatial derivative of the displacement: where the dots are most squeezed, the pressure is highest; where they are pulled apart, the pressure is lowest. We will care about that pressure for the rest of the essay because it is what the receiver actually measures.

From here on, we'll stop drawing the dots and just plot the pressure wave on its own. But every line you see for the next hour is, underneath, a pattern of squeezes and stretches travelling through water at about 1500 metres per second.

How fast it travels

That "about 1500 metres per second" needs a minute of context. Sound in water is roughly four and a half times faster than sound in air — quickly enough that a ping fired at a submarine 1.5 km away returns in two seconds. The exact number depends mildly on the temperature, salinity, and pressure of the water the ping is travelling through, but the dependence is gentle: in the upper ocean, the speed sits somewhere between 1440 and 1540 m/s.

In the figure, a transducer at the centre fires a single ping. The red ring is the wavefront — a two-dimensional slice through the three-dimensional pressure shell expanding around the source. It travels outward at speed c. Drag the temperature slider; in warmer water, c goes up a bit and the ring races out slightly faster.

Everything that follows assumes a single value for c. Real sonar has to cope with c varying with depth (which bends rays in a way that can hide submarines under a thermocline), but that is its own essay.

A single ping

The simplest possible ping is a brief burst of a single tone — a sine wave turned on for a few milliseconds and then turned off. Here is one:

Two observations, each of which will come back:

The spectrum is roughly 1/τ wide. A 10 ms burst is about 100 Hz wide at the base; a 100 ms burst is about 10 Hz wide. That's not a coincidence — it is the fundamental uncertainty principle of signal processing, a purely mathematical statement that applies to every waveform that ever existed. Make it short in time, it spreads in frequency. Make it narrow in frequency, it has to be long in time.

A tone burst is a terrible sonar pulse. It is either too short (almost no energy in the water — you can't hear it come back) or too long (can't tell two close echoes apart — more on this in a minute). We will keep using tone bursts for a few more figures because they're simple, and then replace them with something cleverer.

Echoes from a target

Put a hard object in the ping's path — a rock, a hull, a fish with a swim bladder — and some fraction of the energy bounces back. If the target is at range R0, the wave takes R0/c seconds to reach it, another R0/c to come back, and returns to the transducer at a round-trip time of τd = 2R0/c.

The whole game of active sonar is encoded in this figure. If you can measure τd accurately, you can solve for R0. If you can measure how the echo differs from the outgoing pulse — its frequency content, its time spread, its amplitude — you can also infer whether the target is moving, how big it is, what it might be made of. Everything else in this essay is a refinement of either the "measure τd" step or the "measure how the echo differs" step.

Range from time of flight

Let's do the first one right now. Below is a recording of a transducer: one outgoing burst at t = 0, and somewhere later in the trace, an echo. Drag the dashed ruler until it lines up with the echo. The readout tells you the implied range R = c·t/2.

Two small catches, though. One is that you have to know which blip is the echo — the trace has faint random wiggles in it (noise) and possibly reflections from other objects. The other is that your ability to pin down t precisely is limited by how sharp the echo is, which is limited by the pulse shape. We'll come back to both.

Spreading and absorption

The echo you measured in the last figure was marked confidently on the page, but in the water it is very quiet. Two effects conspire to weaken an acoustic pulse as it travels:

Spreading. The ping's energy is shared over a shell whose area grows as R². By the time the shell reaches 1 km, its surface area is a million times the area at 1 m. Pressure amplitude drops like 1/R, which in decibels is 20 log₁₀ R: a 20 dB loss per decade of range, before you've even considered the water itself.

Absorption. The water molecules themselves convert some of the acoustic energy to heat via a handful of relaxation mechanisms (boron, magnesium sulphate, pure water viscosity). Thorp's formula is a convenient curve fit: a frequency-dependent coefficient α(f), in dB per kilometre, that you add to the spreading loss. The catch is that α grows quickly with frequency.

This is why long-range active sonar lives at low frequencies (hundreds of Hz to a few kHz) and fish-finders or imaging sonars live up in the tens of kHz or higher: they trade range for resolution. Spoiler: the reason higher frequencies give better resolution is coming up next.
