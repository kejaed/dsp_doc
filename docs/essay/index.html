Sonar

How a box on a boat knows there's a submarine four kilometres away, from first principles.

A long, slow read in twenty figures. Drag every slider.

Imagine a steel ball dropped into a still pond. A round ridge of water races outward from where it landed. The ridge carries no water with it — every drop in the pond stays roughly where it was — only the shape of the surface travels. When the ridge reaches the dock at the edge of the pond it slaps the wood, bounces off, and races back the way it came. If you'd been timing it, you could have measured the round trip and worked out how far the dock is.

That is, very nearly, sonar. There is no pond, no ball, and no wooden dock — but underwater there are pressure waves, and there is a transducer that fires them, and there are submarines and rocks and fish that bounce them back. Every step in what follows is a slightly more careful version of the pond.

Before any of that, though, we have to be precise about what is actually travelling between the transducer and the target. It is not water. It is a pressure wave: a moving pattern of squeezes and stretches in the medium. The figure below is a cartoon of one.

Two things are worth noticing here. First, although the pattern is moving steadily from left to right, no single dot ever drifts left or right — each one wobbles in place. Sound is a way for energy and information to cross a medium without carrying any of the medium with it. Second, the pressure profile is exactly the spatial derivative of the displacement: where the dots are most squeezed, the pressure is highest; where they are pulled apart, the pressure is lowest. We will care about that pressure for the rest of the essay because it is what the receiver actually measures.

From here on, we'll stop drawing the dots and just plot the pressure wave on its own. But every line you see for the next hour is, underneath, a pattern of squeezes and stretches travelling through water at about 1500 metres per second.

How fast it travels

That "about 1500 metres per second" needs a minute of context. Sound in water is roughly four and a half times faster than sound in air — quickly enough that a ping fired at a submarine 1.5 km away returns in two seconds. The exact number depends mildly on the temperature, salinity, and pressure of the water the ping is travelling through, but the dependence is gentle: in the upper ocean, the speed sits somewhere between 1440 and 1540 m/s.

In the figure, a transducer at the centre fires a single ping. The red ring is the wavefront — a two-dimensional slice through the three-dimensional pressure shell expanding around the source. It travels outward at speed c. Drag the temperature slider; in warmer water, c goes up a bit and the ring races out slightly faster.

Everything that follows assumes a single value for c. Real sonar has to cope with c varying with depth (which bends rays in a way that can hide submarines under a thermocline), but that is its own essay.

A single ping

The simplest possible ping is a brief burst of a single tone — a sine wave turned on for a few milliseconds and then turned off. Here is one:

Two observations, each of which will come back:

The spectrum is roughly 1/τ wide. A 10 ms burst is about 100 Hz wide at the base; a 100 ms burst is about 10 Hz wide. That's not a coincidence — it is the fundamental uncertainty principle of signal processing, a purely mathematical statement that applies to every waveform that ever existed. Make it short in time, it spreads in frequency. Make it narrow in frequency, it has to be long in time.

A tone burst is a terrible sonar pulse. It is either too short (almost no energy in the water — you can't hear it come back) or too long (can't tell two close echoes apart — more on this in a minute). We will keep using tone bursts for a few more figures because they're simple, and then replace them with something cleverer.

Echoes from a target

Put a hard object in the ping's path — a rock, a hull, a fish with a swim bladder — and some fraction of the energy bounces back. If the target is at range R0, the wave takes R0/c seconds to reach it, another R0/c to come back, and returns to the transducer at a round-trip time of τd = 2R0/c.

The whole game of active sonar is encoded in this figure. If you can measure τd accurately, you can solve for R0. If you can measure how the echo differs from the outgoing pulse — its frequency content, its time spread, its amplitude — you can also infer whether the target is moving, how big it is, what it might be made of. Everything else in this essay is a refinement of either the "measure τd" step or the "measure how the echo differs" step.

Range from time of flight

Let's do the first one right now. Below is a recording of a transducer: one outgoing burst at t = 0, and somewhere later in the trace, an echo. Drag the dashed ruler until it lines up with the echo. The readout tells you the implied range R = c·t/2.

Two small catches, though. One is that you have to know which blip is the echo — the trace has faint random wiggles in it (noise) and possibly reflections from other objects. The other is that your ability to pin down t precisely is limited by how sharp the echo is, which is limited by the pulse shape. We'll come back to both.

Spreading and absorption

The echo you measured in the last figure was marked confidently on the page, but in the water it is very quiet. Two effects conspire to weaken an acoustic pulse as it travels:

Spreading. The ping's energy is shared over a shell whose area grows as R². By the time the shell reaches 1 km, its surface area is a million times the area at 1 m. Pressure amplitude drops like 1/R, which in decibels is 20 log₁₀ R: a 20 dB loss per decade of range, before you've even considered the water itself.

Absorption. The water molecules themselves convert some of the acoustic energy to heat via a handful of relaxation mechanisms (boron, magnesium sulphate, pure water viscosity). Thorp's formula is a convenient curve fit: a frequency-dependent coefficient α(f), in dB per kilometre, that you add to the spreading loss. The catch is that α grows quickly with frequency.

This is why long-range active sonar lives at low frequencies (hundreds of Hz to a few kHz) and fish-finders or imaging sonars live up in the tens of kHz or higher: they trade range for resolution. Spoiler: the reason higher frequencies give better resolution is coming up next.

The resolution problem

Suppose two targets sit close together — say a couple of dozen metres apart. Each one returns its own echo. The transducer doesn't know there are two of them; it just sees the sum of two pulses arriving back-to-back. If the pulse is short, the two echoes arrive cleanly separated in time and you can count them. If the pulse is long, they overlap, and what you see is one fat blob.

So the obvious move is to use a very short pulse. Make τ tiny and you can resolve targets that are inches apart. But there's the catch we already met in §3: a pulse that is short in time is wide in frequency, and short in time also means it carries very little energy. We can't actually transmit a delta function and hope to hear its echo; the receiver would just see noise. What we actually want is a pulse that is simultaneously long in duration (so it carries energy) and broad in bandwidth (so it resolves close targets). That sounds like a contradiction. It isn't, quite, and the next section explains the way out.

Bandwidth IS resolution

Here is the cleaner way to state the resolution rule. After we've done the right processing on the receive side (the matched filter, which we'll meet in §11), the achievable range resolution is set entirely by the pulse's bandwidth B:

Δr = c / (2B).

That's it. Whatever shape the pulse has, however long or short it is, what matters at the end is how much frequency it spans. A pulse with 1 kHz of bandwidth gives 75 cm of resolution in water; 10 kHz gives 7.5 cm; 100 Hz gives 7.5 m. The same figure as before, but now the slider is bandwidth and the bracket on the plot shows you Δr directly.

So we want bandwidth. Lots of it. And we still want enough total energy to survive the trip out to the target and back. The trick is the chirp.

The chirp trick

Instead of holding a single tone for τ seconds, sweep the frequency smoothly from f0 to f0+B over the same τ. The resulting waveform is called a linear-FM chirp, or LFM. It looks like a long, smooth squeak that rises (or falls) in pitch. Birds use them. Bats use them. Submarines use them.

The chirp gives us both: τ can be long (carrying lots of energy) and B can be large (giving fine range resolution after processing). The processing that turns the long, slow squeak into a sharp peak is the matched filter, which gets its own section in a moment. First, a way of seeing what's actually happening to the chirp's frequency over time.

A spectrogram of the chirp

A spectrogram is a way of plotting a signal as a heatmap with time across and frequency up. It's just a stack of short-time spectra computed every few milliseconds, glued together; the colour of each pixel is "how much energy is at this frequency at this time". For a tone, the spectrogram is a horizontal stripe. For a chirp, it's a tilted line — the slope of the line is exactly B/τ.

We have spent five sections on what to send. The remainder of the essay is about what to do with what comes back.

The matched filter

We send a long chirp to put energy in the water; we want a short, sharp peak on the receive side so we can pin down the target's range to within a metre or two. The piece of mathematics that does that conversion is the matched filter. It is the most important idea in this essay, and once you've seen it work, you understand most of why active sonar exists in its modern form at all.

The matched filter is, simply, a cross-correlation of the received signal with a copy of the transmit waveform. Slide the reference chirp along the receive trace; at each shift, multiply the two point-by-point and sum. When the shift lines up exactly with the actual delay of the echo, every wiggle in the echo lines up with the corresponding wiggle in the reference, the products are all positive, and the sum is huge. At any other shift, the wiggles are misaligned and the products mostly cancel — the sum is tiny. The output of the cross-correlation, as a function of shift, has one big spike where the echo actually lives and is small everywhere else.

Notice three things. First, the peak in the bottom plot is at the target's true delay, regardless of what the input looks like. Second, the peak's width is set by the chirp's bandwidth, not its duration — which is exactly the bandwidth-equals-resolution claim from §8 finally delivered. Third, the area under the spike is the same as the area under the long echo before processing — the matched filter rearranges energy from a long, low pulse into a short, tall one. It doesn't manufacture energy; it just concentrates it.

What noise does

So far the receiver has been quiet — only the echo, only the target. In practice, every receiver hears a low background of random pressure fluctuations: the ocean's own noise (waves, biology, ships), the electronic noise floor of the amplifier, and so on. We model all of that as a Gaussian random process added to the received signal.

This is where the matched filter starts to look like magic. The bottom slider controls the input SNR: positive numbers mean the echo dominates the noise; negative numbers mean the noise dominates the echo. At low SNR the raw receive trace looks like static — you can't pick out the chirp by eye at all. But the matched-filter output below it still has the target peak.

Why does this work? The matched filter is provably the optimal linear operation for detecting a known waveform in white Gaussian noise — it maximises the peak-output-SNR among all linear filters. The intuition is that it weights every received sample by how much "signal" we expect to see there, and it happens that the optimal weighting is just a copy of the signal itself. There's a careful proof; for our purposes it's enough to know that you cannot do better with a fixed pulse shape and a Gaussian noise floor.

With the matched filter we have everything we need to find a stationary target: send the chirp, correlate the echo with a reference, look for a peak, read off its delay, divide by 2/c, and we have a range. The next thing to ask is what happens if the target isn't stationary.

A target on the move

If the target is moving toward or away from us at a radial speed v, each cycle of the returning wave is squeezed (toward) or stretched (away) relative to what we sent. The echo carrier shifts in frequency by

fd = −2·v·fc/c.

The factor of two is because the wave makes the trip twice — out, then back — and each leg picks up the same fractional Doppler. The minus sign is a convention choice (we'll take positive v = receding, which gives a downward shift). Plugging in numbers: at fc = 1 kHz, a target receding at 5 m/s shifts the echo by about −7 Hz. Small in absolute terms; large enough to detect easily.

The Doppler shift is simultaneously a problem and an opportunity. A problem because the matched filter we just built was tuned to the exact transmit frequency content — feed it a Doppler-shifted echo and the correlation peak shrinks (the wiggles no longer line up exactly). An opportunity because if we can measure fd we get the target's radial velocity for free, on top of its range.

The Doppler spectrum

Measuring fd from the echo of a single short ping is hard, because the ping itself only has so much frequency resolution (uncertainty principle, again — its spectrum is roughly 1/τ wide). At τ = 30 ms our spectrum is ~30 Hz wide. A ±5 Hz Doppler shift moves the whole spectrum by less than a single FFT bin; you would not notice.

The trick to making Doppler measurable from short pings is to fire many of them, evenly spaced in time, and use the long observation interval to get the frequency resolution we lack within a single ping. That's what the next three sections build up.

A train of pings

Modern sonars don't fire one ping; they fire a sequence — every PRI seconds (PRI for pulse repetition interval) for as long as the operator wants. Each ping comes back, the receiver matched-filters it, and the result is one row in a stack of receive traces. Two natural axes appear: fast time across each row (where in the row is the echo — that's range), and slow time down the stack (which ping number — that turns out to be Doppler).

At a fixed range bin (a vertical column of the matrix), the echo's value from ping to ping is not constant — it picks up the inter-pulse Doppler phase exp(j 2π fd · k · PRI) for the k-th ping. The rate at which that phase advances along the slow-time axis is the Doppler frequency. We just need to do an FFT down each column.

Coherent integration

Before doing the column FFT, here is a closely related trick that's worth a figure of its own: coherent averaging. Sum the matched-filter outputs from N pings (treating them as complex signals — phase and all) and divide by N. The target peak is in the same place every time, so it adds constructively; the noise has random phase, so it adds destructively. After N pulses, target amplitude is unchanged; noise standard deviation drops by √N. The peak-to-noise ratio improves by 10·log₁₀(N) dB.

Coherent integration is what we get for stacking pings that all look the same. But pings from a moving target don't all look the same — they have a slowly rotating phase. If we could un-rotate them and then sum, we'd get the same gain. The way to do that, for an unknown velocity, is to try every plausible Doppler shift simultaneously, which is just an FFT.

The range–Doppler map

The combination of "stack N pings" and "FFT each column" produces the range–Doppler map: a 2-D image with range across, radial velocity up, and brightness equal to how much energy is at that range-velocity cell. A target at (R0, v) shows up as a bright dot at the corresponding pixel; everywhere else the map is just integrated noise.

Notice that PRI and bandwidth play parallel roles for Doppler and range: bandwidth × duration sets fast-time (range) resolution, and N · PRI (the total observation time) sets slow-time (Doppler) resolution. The rest of sonar engineering is largely a fight between these two — fine Doppler wants long observation, fine range wants wide bandwidth, and you can never have everything you want.

With the RD map, we know where a target is in the range-velocity grid if there is one. The remaining question is: how bright does a cell have to be before we believe it's actually a target and not a noise fluctuation?

When to declare a target

The simplest answer is to set a threshold on the matched-filter output. Anything above the line is "target", anything below is "no target". Drag the threshold slider in the figure below; the figure tells you how the decision goes.

This is the central tradeoff of detection theory: probability of detection (Pd) vs probability of false alarm (Pfa). Drag the slider and you can pick any operating point on the curve, but you cannot reduce both at once for a fixed input SNR.

A constant threshold has a more practical problem too: the noise floor is rarely constant in range. Reverberation from the seafloor is loud at short range and soft at long range. A threshold that gives the right false-alarm rate at 4 km would give thousands of false alarms at 200 m. We need a threshold that tracks the local noise.

CFAR — an adaptive threshold

The standard fix is called constant false-alarm rate detection — CFAR. The idea: for each cell-under-test, estimate the local noise power from a small ring of neighbouring cells (the "training cells"), leaving a few "guard cells" around the cell-under-test so the target itself doesn't pollute its own noise estimate. Multiply the local noise estimate by a constant α and you have your threshold for that cell. Repeat for every cell. The threshold curve adapts to whatever noise floor it's running over.

For exponentially distributed noise power and N training cells, the formula for α that gives a target false-alarm rate of Pfa is α = N (Pfa−1/N − 1). Both sliders below — design Pfa and the number of training cells — feed into this.

CFAR is what every working sonar uses, in one variant or another (cell- averaging, ordered-statistic, OS-CFAR for clutter edges, etc.). It replaces a brittle, hand-tuned threshold with a knob that reads "how much false-alarm rate can I tolerate" — which is the right question to be asking.
